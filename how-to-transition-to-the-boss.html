How do I transition from colleague to boss?

July 02, 2024
blog

Here, Jo advises a reader who has recently been promoted and wants to stay friends with her old colleagues, while earning their respect as a boss.

Dear Jo,

I’ve recently been promoted to senior manager at the branding agency where I work. I’ve been here for a long time – eight years – with many of the same colleagues throughout my career, so it feels like a big, and well-earned, step up for me.

Because I’ve been at the company for quite a while, I’ve formed close friendships with many co-workers at my level, some of whom will now be reporting to me. How do I make the transition from colleague to boss without alienating people?

Yours thankfully,

Ophelia

Jo says...

Dear Ophelia,

First of all, many congratulations on the promotion! It’s great that your company is rewarding your efforts and loyalty, and I’m sure your move up is very well deserved.

Whether you are being promoted internally or starting in a completely new role, it’s normal for there to be a period of adjustment, which is often massively underestimated. In reality, it takes around a year for someone to adapt to a brand new company and around six months for an internal transition to settle in. It’s important to allow yourself this time to navigate the inevitable changes – and this includes any new team dynamics, like the ones you have mentioned.

I’m sure your colleagues are delighted that you have been given this opportunity and hopefully recognise how hard you’ve worked to get there. So, let’s start by assuming ‘good will’ and take it from there.

There are many ways to be a successful leader, and much depends on who you are, your attitude and the company culture. But let’s focus on a few of the qualities that will help you smoothly transition into this new role, while also maintaining the friendship and respect of your colleagues.

1/ Be consistent

Since you have been at the company for a long time, you will have built a personal brand for which you are recognised. Even though you are taking on new responsibilities, try to maintain this as much as you can.

2/ Be present

More seniority generally means you’ll have less time and more pressure. We know that communication is key to all successful relationships. So as much as you can, try not to let this additional workload prevent you from being there for your team or your colleagues when they need your support, even if you no longer have time for the daily dating debriefs!

3/ Be solutions focused

It’s your ‘can do’ attitude that has likely contributed to this promotion, so use it as much as you can, even if some of your colleagues are struggling to make the transition with you. Your willingness to seek solutions will work out well for everyone in the end.

4/ Be humble

It’s easy to let a new job title go to your head, especially if it’s something you’ve wanted for a while, so be mindful and respectful of those around you. And remember, you never know who may have wanted to be in your shoes and is now having to manage their own disappointment.

5/ Have confidence in yourself

There’s nothing worse than leader who crowd funds every decision! Just because these people are your friends, it doesn’t mean you have to ask for their input on everything. Go with your gut, it’s very rarely wrong.

6/ Respect boundaries

Your new position will give you access to more confidential information. Don’t be tempted to share any of this with your colleagues just to curry favour. It doesn’t help them and could get you into all sorts of trouble.

Finally, just be yourself and take each day as it comes. You’re bound to make a few mistakes along the way, and that’s ok. Mistakes, although uncomfortable at the time, are the best way to learn, so embrace the change and enjoy the journey.

Good luck!
