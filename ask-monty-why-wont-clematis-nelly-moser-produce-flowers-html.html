Why won't my Clematis 'Nelly Moser' produce any flowers?

By Monty Don for MailOnline
Updated: 15:34 EST, 26 September 2010

View
comments

Climber: 'Nelly Mosers' produce large flowers in late spring and early summer

I have a Clematis 'Nelly Moser', which I planted under a rowan tree hoping it would climb up it. After the first year a few flowers appeared but there have been no more in the number of years since. Why?

B. Caldwell, Cumbernauld, North Lanarkshire

This is likely to be one of two problems, or a combination of both. 'Nelly Moser' is a group 2 clematis, producing large flowers in late spring and early summer. These are formed on wood grown the previous year. So if you prune it in spring you will remove flowering buds.

Any pruning of these early flowering clematis should be done immediately after they flower. But another reason may well be because it is too dry. If you plant any climber close to a tree it will be competing with it for moisture, and a clematis needs lots of water. Finally, clematis do best on alkaline soils, whereas rowans thrive on ericaceous soil. One of the two will always struggle.

I have a 60-year-old pear tree. Over the past three years there have been plenty of fruits, but when they get to an inch in size the leaves develop orange patches and the fruits start to fall. All the leaves become infected. I have tried numerous treatments but to no avail. What can I do?

J. Williams, Harrow, Middlesex

The loss of fruits at an immature stage is a result of poor pollination (you need a nearby pear tree to be flowering at the same time), but if the dropped fruit had been hollowed out by grubs, then this is down to pear midge. However, the orange patches sound like the result of grazing by the pear slugworm. This is the slug-like larvae of the sawfly, which eats the upper surface of the leaf, causing patches. Good ventilation will help, so prune the tree to allow in lots of light and air.
